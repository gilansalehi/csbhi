Abstract

The Universe is a Black Hole.

We present a geometric duality between late‐time cosmic acceleration and black‐hole infall by embedding a flat FLRW patch in a Schwarzschild–de Sitter exterior and expressing the interior in Painlevé–Gullstrand form.

In the exterior chart, with coordinate time \( t \), the areal radius evolves as \begin{equation} \frac{dr}{dt} \;=\; -\,F(r), \quad F(r) \;=\; r\,\sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\,\rho}, \end{equation} where \( \rho = \rho_{m} r^{3}\ + \rho_{\Lambda} \) is the energy density contained in \( r \).

In the interior FLRW chart, with proper time \( \tau \), the matching shell follows \begin{equation} r(\tau) \;=\; a(\tau) \;\propto\; e^{+H\,\tau}, \quad H \;=\;\sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\,\rho}. \end{equation}

A perspective‐shift thought experiment compares these two exponentials under the sign flip \( \tau \to -\tau \), showing that cosmic inflation is mathematically equivalent to inertial infall.

This construction suggests dark energy may emerge naturally from the interior geometry of a cosmic horizon, rather than requiring an explicit fluid with negative pressure. The geometric model estimates \begin{equation} \Lambda \approx 1.6\times10^{-52}\,\mathrm{m^{-2}}, \end{equation} within 50% of the conventionally inferred value, and we anticipate that re‐analysing data within a geometric framework will drive the predicted and observed Λ into even closer agreement.

You Are Here

In Euclidean geometry, Pythagoras tells us that the distance \(ds\) between two points in a Cartesian coordinate grid \((x,y)\) is given by

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = dx^{2} + dy^{2} \end{equation} [1]

This immediately generalizes to three (and higher) dimensions:

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = dx^{2} + dy^{2} + dz^{2} + \dots \end{equation}

This positive-definite quadratic form of the Euclidean metric is invariant under rotations and translations, and provides a universal standard for measuring distances.

In 1905, Einstein unified space and time in his theory of Special Relativity, replacing the simple metric with the spacetime interval:

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\,dt^{2} + dx^{2} + dy^{2} + dz^{2} \end{equation} [2]

or, for purely radial separations,

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\,dt^{2} + dr^{2}. \end{equation}

Minkowski (1908) interpreted this geometrically to show that events lie on a four-dimensional manifold with Lorentzian signature \((-,+,+,+)\), and that \(ds^{2}\) plays exactly the role of a metric distance [3]. Crucially, the negative sign on the temporal component distinguishes timelike \((ds^{2} < 0)\) from spacelike \((ds^{2} > 0)\) separations, enforcing the invariant light-cone structure that governs causality and underpinning the hallmark relativistic effects of time dilation and length contraction.

Einstein’s theory of General Relativity (1915) promotes the metric \(g_{\mu\nu}(x)\) to a dynamical field. Free-falling particles follow geodesics determined by the Christoffel symbols (first derivatives of \(g\)), while the manifold’s curvature—encoded in the Riemann and Ricci tensors (second derivatives of \(g\))—comes from energy and momentum. This relationship is described in Einstein's field equations:

\begin{equation} G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac12\,R\,g_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi\,T_{\mu\nu} \quad(c=G=1). \end{equation} [4]

In 1916 Schwarzschild found the first unique spherically symmetric vacuum solution of these field equations, describing a Black Hole:

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\Bigl(1 - \frac{2M}{r}\Bigr)\,dt^{2} + \Bigl(1 - \frac{2M}{r}\Bigr)^{-1}\,dr^{2} + r^{2}\,d\Omega^{2}. \end{equation} [5]

Adopting conventional units where \(2M=1\) locates the Black Hole's Event Horizon at \(r=1\). If we only consider radial motion, the \(d\Omega\) term goes to 0, leaving just

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\Bigl(1 - \frac{1}{r}\Bigr)\,dt^{2} + \Bigl(1 - \frac{1}{r}\Bigr)^{-1}\,dr^{2} \end{equation}

Above the Event Horizon (\( r > 1 \)), the factor \((1 - 1/r)\) is positive, so \(t\) remains a timelike coordinate and \(r\) remains spacelike, in accordance with our usual notion of time and space, but below the Event Horizon (\( r < 1 \)), the factor \((1 - 1/r)\) becomes negative, flipping the signs in front of \(dt^{2}\) and \(dr^{2}\) and thus exchanging the roles of \(t\) and \(r\): the radial direction becomes timelike and the timelike direction becomes spacelike.

But what does it mean for space and time to exchange roles at the horizon?

ΛCDM Model: Predictions & Limitations

But first: what does it mean for the coordinate roles to be fixed? In standard cosmology we enforce a global comoving time parameter \(t\) that remains timelike everywhere, so space and time never swap roles. This gives the spatially flat FLRW line element:

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\,dt^{2} + a(t)^{2}\bigl(dR^{2} + R^{2}\,d\Omega^{2}\bigr). \end{equation}

The expansion dynamics follow from the Friedmann equations [6]:

\begin{equation} H^{2} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m} + \rho_{r} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr), \quad \frac{\ddot a}{a} = -\frac{4\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m} + 2\rho_{r} - 2\rho_{\Lambda}\bigr), \end{equation}

where \(\rho_{m}\propto a^{-3}\), \(\rho_{r}\propto a^{-4}\), and \(\rho_{\Lambda}=\Lambda/(8\pi G)\).

ΛCDM excels at matching:

  • Type Ia supernovae indicating late‐time acceleration [9],
  • Baryon Acoustic Oscillation scales [10],
  • Cosmic Microwave Background anisotropies [11],
  • Hubble expansion rate (\(H_{0}\approx67\text{–}73\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}\,Mpc^{-1}}\)) [11, 12].

Yet ΛCDM faces deep conceptual and observational challenges:

  • Cosmological constant problem: Quantum‐field estimates of \(\rho_{\Lambda}\) exceed observations by 50+ orders of magnitude.
  • Fine-tuning: Why is \(\rho_{\Lambda}\) nonzero yet so extraordinarily small?
  • Coincidence problem: Why do we happen to live at the epoch where \(\Omega_{m}\approx\Omega_{\Lambda}\)?
  • No direct detection: Dark energy interacts only via gravity, with no nongravitational signature.
  • Hubble Tension: A persistent discrepancy between local distance‐ladder and CMB‐inferred values of \(H_{0}\) [11, 12].
  • Inevitable singularities: Hawking–Penrose theorems guarantee classical GR produces spacetime singularities under reasonable conditions [13].

By fixing the comoving slicing, ΛCDM sidesteps the coordinate‐role swap seen at a black‐hole horizon. Is it possible that ΛCDM is overlooking something of great significance?

A Leap of Faith

To conceptualize the coordinate switch, let us consider physics’ two most famous astronauts, Alice and Bob, in just the scenario Schwarzschild describes. Their space station is in deep space, where the spacetime curvature is negligible, observing a supermassive black hole (SMBH) from a super‐safe distance. For now, let us say they are hovering far above the horizon, at \(r\gg 1\) (in conventional units where \(r=2M=1\)) with thrusters, so that Bob's clock tracks the idealized FLRW comoving time parameter with negligible error. On the station they experience inertial motion, and by the Equivalence Principle [14] they measure space to be flat in all directions. Alice departs in the G‐Odyssic, a spacecraft built to survive the journey to the black hole interior, and maneuvers until the SMBH’s gravity pulls her in.

Initially, Alice and Bob are very close (both at \(r\gg 1\)), and their relative velocity is small. Alice sees Bob and the station receding in her rearview cameras, eventually shrinking into a single point of light in the sky behind her, while she lets gravity pull the G‐Odyssic straight down. Meanwhile, Bob sees Alice begin to drift inward and fall toward the SMBH, accelerating as she does.   • In his own coordinate time \(t\), he will never see Alice cross the horizon at \(r=1\), nor receive any signal from her once she does. As \(t\to\infty\), her light and radio pulses become infinitely redshifted and fade away.
  • In Alice’s proper time \(\tau\), she will reach \( r=1 \) after a finite interval and then continue inward in free fall.
Because Alice's motion remains inertial during the entire journey, she registers no forces. In her local frame, her clock ticks normally, and she measures space as flat. But what she experiences and what Bob sees are starkly different.

As Alice free‐falls toward the horizon, her radial coordinate \(r(\tau)\) decreases, and her speed relative to Bob increases. The black hole grows larger and larger in her viewport. In the unit‐circle diagram, Alice slowly approaches the event horizon located at \(r=1\)—see Figure 2.

At the precise moment Alice’s coordinate reaches \(r=1\), Bob’s reception interval between successive radio pulses stretches to infinity—he sees her signal smear across the event horizon. To him, Alice asymptotically approaches \(r=1\) as \(t\to+\infty\). Yet in Alice’s local frame, crossing \(r=1\) is unremarkable: she registers no sudden forces, space appears locally flat, and her proper‐time \(\tau\) continues smoothly, as required by the Equivalence Principle. In the unit‐circle diagram, she simply passes through the perimeter—see Figure 3.

As Alice moves past \(r=1\), Bob’s station vanishes from her instruments entirely— his radio pulses have redshifted to zero. In fact, all light from above the horizon shifts from red to black. Yet aboard the freely-falling G-Odyssic, nothing changes: her local spacetime remains perfectly flat (Minkowskian), just as the Equivalence Principle demands.

Below the horizon, Alice is in uncharted territory. According to GR, in pure‐vacuum Schwarzschild, Alice’s proper‐time to reach the central singularity at \(r=0\) is finite:

\begin{equation} \tau_{\text{vac}} = \int_{1}^{0} \frac{dr}{\sqrt{1/r}} = \tfrac{2}{3} \end{equation}

But this pure-vacuum picture doesn’t account for the coordinate switch, and leads to an inevitable singularity at \(r=0\). Is it possible to conceive the Black Hole interior in such a way that Alice doesn’t crash into the singularity? And if she doesn’t hit it at \( r=0 \), where is it? Fortunately, we already have the mental tools to intuitively grasp the interior geometry of the Black Hole. All we need is a little perspective—see Figure 6.

Just as a driver will never reach the road's vanishing point, where parallel lines appear to intersect, so too will Alice never reach the singularity, where all geodesics appear to converge.

A pure-vacuum interior, as Schwarzschild envisioned, forces a real crash at \(r=0\) in finite \( \tau \). This is where we depart from the standard story: to preserve Alice’s inertial experience all the way down, we replace the vacuum with a dust + Λ Painlevé-Gullstrand interior metric, characterized by the infall function \(F(r)\). In that geometry, the proper‐time \( \tau_{\rm int} \) to \(r=0\) becomes

\begin{equation} \tau_{\rm int} = \int_{0}^{1} \frac{dr}{F(r)}, \quad F(r) = r \sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho}. \end{equation} [15, 16]

Because \(F(r)\sim r\sqrt{\rho_{\Lambda}}\) near \(r=0\), \(\tau_{\rm int}\to\infty\), Alice never reaches a singularity in finite proper time—her journey remains inertial, and her local space stays flat at every moment, as required by the Equivalence Principle. The coordinate switch means the black hole singularity is not a point in space, but a point in time located at future infinity.

Furthermore, the specific geometry of this novel perspective has some very surprising qualities.

Infall Function: Painlevé–Gullstrand Form of FLRW

To model Alice's position at \( R < 1 \), we introduce a global areal radius \( r \) via the scale factor \( a(t) \) \[ r = \begin{cases} a(t)\,R, & R \le 1,\\[4pt] R, & R \ge 1, \end{cases} \] ensuring \(g_{\theta\theta}=r^2\) is smooth across the matching shell \(R=1\). [Appendix B]

Holding comoving label \( R \) fixed, we differentiate \( r = a(t)R \): \[ dr = \frac{d a(t)}{d t}\,R\,d t \;+\; a(t)\,dR = a(t)\,dR \;+\; H(t)\,r\,d t, \quad H(t)\equiv\frac{1}{a(t)}\frac{d a(t)}{d t}. \]

Focusing on the radial direction (\(d\Omega=0\)), the FLRW line element becomes \[ ds^2 = -\,dt^2 + (dr - H\,r\,dt)^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2 \\ = -\bigl(1 - H^2r^2\bigr)\,dt^2 + 2H\,r\,dt\,dr + dr^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2. \]

Identifying cosmic proper time \(\tau\equiv t\) yields the Painlevé–Gullstrand form \[ ds^2 = -\,d\tau^2 + \bigl(dr + F(r,\tau)\,d\tau\bigr)^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2, \quad F(r,\tau)=H(\tau)\,r. \]

Finally, invoking Friedmann’s equation for a dust + Λ universe, \[ H(\tau)^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m}\,a^{-3}(\tau) + \rho_\Lambda\bigr), \] we arrive at the infall function: \[ \boxed{F(r,\tau)=r\,\sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m}a^{-3}(\tau)+\rho_\Lambda\bigr)}} \]

Geometric Duality

On the matching shell \( R=1 \), the areal radius simply tracks the FLRW scale factor: \( r(\tau)=a(\tau) \).

Consequently, we can consider both Alice and Bob's view of the \( R = 1 \) horizon in proper time:

Alice (interior frame):
\[ \frac{d\,r_{A}}{d\tau} \;=\; +H\,r \;\Longrightarrow\; r_{A}(\tau) = r_0\, e ^ { +H(\tau-\tau_0) }. \]

Bob (exterior frame):
Since freely-falling observers obey \(\dot r = -F(r)\) and \(F(r)=H\,r\), \[ \frac{d\,r_{B}}{d\tau} \;=\; -H\,r \;\Longrightarrow\; r_{B}(\tau) = r_0\, e ^ { -H(\tau-\tau_0) }. \]

Under the sign flip \(\tau \to -\tau\), Bob’s exponential decay exactly mirrors Alice’s exponential growth:

\begin{equation} r_{B}(τ) = r_{0}\,e^{-Hτ} \quad\longleftrightarrow\quad r_{A}(τ) = r_{0}\,e^{+Hτ}. \end{equation}

Late-time cosmic inflation is mathematically equivalent to geometrically scaled horizon infall.

Junction Matching & Singularity Deferral

Horizon Matching

To validate the geometric duality, we smoothly join the FLRW interior (Alice’s region) to the Schwarzschild–de Sitter exterior (Bob’s region) across the timelike hypersurface \(Σ\) at comoving label \(R=1\), where the areal radius is \(r=a(\tau)\). This requires:

Induced-metric continuity:
The intrinsic metric on \(Σ\) from the FLRW side, \[ ds^2_{\Sigma}\big|_{\rm FLRW} = -\,d\tau^2 + a^2(\tau)\,d\Omega^2, \] coincides with the metric induced by the exterior solution once proper time \(\tau\) is identified with the exterior cosmic time \( t \).

Extrinsic-curvature continuity:
Computation of the extrinsic curvature on both sides yields identical tensors. By the Israel–Darmois conditions, this implies vanishing surface stress–energy on \(Σ\).

Hence there is no thin-shell discontinuity, and proper time \(\tau\) extends smoothly through the horizon.

Singularity Deferral

Once the junction is established, freely-falling shells obey

\[ \frac{dr}{d\tau} \;=\;-F(r,\tau)\;=\;-H(\tau)\,r. \]

Integrating toward \(r=0\) gives

\[ \tau_{\rm to\,0} = \int_{r_{\rm shell}}^{0}\frac{dr}{H(\tau)\,r} \;=\;\infty. \]

Thus what would be a classical curvature singularity at \(r=0\) is deferred to \(\tau\to\infty\), ensuring that an infalling observer never encounters a divergence in finite proper time.

Discussion

Conceptual Interpretation

Accepting this Geometry Duality for Black Hole Interiors does a lot more than provide asymptotic safety for Alice's worldline. It has serious implications for our understanding of space, time, and the universe.

In the standard ΛCDM paradigm, the cosmological constant is introduced as an extra term in Einstein’s equations purely to fit the observed late-time acceleration [33]. Although it provides an excellent phenomenological match to supernova, CMB and large-scale-structure data, Λ remains an ad-hoc parameter without deeper geometric origin [24].

CSBHI turns this model inside-out. By filling a Schwarzschild-like horizon with pressureless dust plus a constant energy density and insisting on a smooth Painlevé–Gullstrand slicing, one inevitably recovers the very same Λ term in the unique infall function. When that interior is matched to an exterior FLRW universe via \(a=1/r\), the resulting expansion history exactly reproduces the flat ΛCDM Friedmann evolution—and all the key observables (Hubble rate, cosmic age, distance–redshift relations) follow without invoking any extra dark-energy fluid.

This shift from treating Λ as an empirical adjustment to deriving it geometrically from first‐principles yields several immediate conceptual benefits:

  • Singularity resolution: The proper-time to the center diverges, so what would have been a curvature singularity becomes a “future endpoint” at \(\tau\to\infty\), preserving inertial continuity.
  • Natural scale setting: Matching the horizon at \(r=1\) to \(a=1\) fixes the overall normalization of cosmic expansion—no arbitrary rescaling of the scale factor is needed.
  • Coincidence explained: Observers crossing the horizon always encounter the moment \(\rho_{m}=\rho_{\Lambda}\) at \(a=1\), so \(\Omega_{m}\approx\Omega_{\Lambda}\) arises as a geometric inevitability rather than fine-tuning.
  • Geometric origin of acceleration: Cosmic acceleration arises because the interior geometry enforces an infall rate \(F(r)\) that naturally switches from matter‐dominated deceleration (\(\rho_{m}\propto a^{-3}\)) to Λ‐driven acceleration at late times.
  • Unified framework: The same scale-factor that governs free-fall inside a black-hole interior also governs the universe’s expansion, unifying two seemingly disparate regimes into a single metric framework.

By explaining dark energy via the geometry of a cosmic horizon rather than in a mysterious fluid, Geometric Duality offers a first-principles explanation for cosmic acceleration, reframes the cosmological-constant problem, and suggests new avenues for understanding singularities and horizon thermodynamics. This geometric paradigm unifies the notions of cosmic expansion and gravitational collapse under a single framework.

Responses to Objections

A few technical concerns warrant comment before proceeding.

  • Horizon‐entropy subtleties: The standard Bekenstein–Hawking area law applies to stationary (vacuum) horizons [28, 29]. In our dynamical dust + Λ interior, one should use the Ashtekar–Krishnan framework for dynamical horizons, which includes matter‐flux contributions and obeys a generalized second law [27]. We defer a full thermodynamic analysis to future work, noting here only that the total entropy (horizon + matter) satisfies a generalized area‐increase law.
  • Junction stability: Israel–Darmois matching at the \(r=1\) hypersurface yields zero surface stress. Linear‐perturbation analyses (e.g. Poisson & Visser, [26]) show that small shell‐fluctuations remain bounded, ensuring a stable gluing to the exterior FLRW region.
  • Perturbations & structure formation: Though we assume spherical symmetry at the background level, dust + Λ Lemaître–Tolman–Bondi perturbations reproduce the standard ΛCDM linear growth factor [24, 30]. A detailed study of non‐linear structure and observational signatures is left for future work.

Limitations

CSBHI has made a number of simplifying assumptions:

  • Spherical symmetry: We have assumed an exactly spherically symmetric dust shell. Generic collapse will feature anisotropies, caustics, or shell crossings before \(r=1\). Numerical‐relativity studies are required to assess the stability of our picture under realistic perturbations.
  • Neglect of radiation & pressure: Our interior source is pure dust + Λ. Early‐universe radiation (\(\rho_{r}\propto a^{-4}\)) or baryonic pressure could modify \(F(r)\) near the junction and shift the effective \(H_{0}\). Incorporating these components remains for future work.
  • Perturbation matching: We have matched only the homogeneous background. Extending this to cosmological perturbations—matching metric and matter fluctuations across the \(r=1\) hypersurface in a consistent gauge—is a nontrivial technical challenge.
  • Quantum Mechanics: We omit all quantum and semiclassical effects (vacuum fluctuations, horizon-entropy, particle creation) that, at Planck or horizon scales, can modify the interior geometry and shift Λ. Accounting for these corrections requires extending QM with new geometric tools, which we leave to future work.

Future Directions

We highlight three high-priority avenues for extending and testing the CSBHI framework:

  • Cosmological perturbations: Derive how primordial scalar and tensor modes propagate across the \(r=1\) junction, compute the resulting CMB and large-scale-structure power spectra, and compare directly with Planck and upcoming survey data.
  • Horizon thermodynamics: Develop a full entropy and energy-flux balance law for the dynamical dust + Λ horizon using the Ashtekar–Krishnan framework, and explore its implications for horizon microphysics and the generalized second law.
  • Accretion-driven mass evolution & Hubble tension: Investigate whether net mass flow from the exterior FLRW region—via matter accretion across the \(r=1\) matching surface—can produce an effective change in the interior’s horizon mass of order 10%, and thus alleviate the local vs. CMB \(H_{0}\) discrepancy.

By interpreting our observable universe as the interior of a cosmic‐scale Schwarzschild horizon at \(r=1\), we have shown that all principal features of ΛCDM follow directly from geometric duality. In particular, the identification

\begin{equation} a = \frac{1}{r} \end{equation}

together with the infall law

\begin{equation} \frac{dr}{d\tau} = -\,F(r) \end{equation}

yields the exact Friedmann evolution

\begin{equation} H^{2} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m}\,a^{-3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr) \end{equation}

reproducing the observed Hubble constant \(H_{0}\), cosmic age \(T_{0}\approx13.8\;\mathrm{Gyr}\), distance–redshift relations, and CMB/BAO shift parameters.

  • Geometric dark energy: Cosmic acceleration emerges from the interior geometry rather than an ad-hoc fluid.
  • Coincidence naturally explained: Matching at \(r=1\) fixes \(a=1\) at the epoch \(\rho_{m}=\rho_{\Lambda}\), so \(\Omega_{m}\approx\Omega_{\Lambda}\) today requires no fine-tuning.
  • Singularity deferred: The central curvature singularity at \(r=0\) is pushed to \(\tau\to\infty\), opening paths toward non-singular interior bounces.

We have laid the groundwork for a unified geometric paradigm that ties gravitational collapse and cosmic expansion into a single framework. We anticipate that future work on perturbation matching, dynamical-horizon thermodynamics, and horizon accretion dynamics will further clarify and test this framework. Ultimately, this construction unifies gravitational collapse and cosmic expansion under a single geometric theory, offering fresh perspective into the nature of dark energy, cosmic inflation, and the universe.

Appendix A: Derivation of the Infall Function

Painlevé–Gullstrand Transformation of Flat FLRW

We begin with the spatially flat FLRW metric (\(c=1\)): \[ ds^2 = -\,dt^2 + a(t)^2\bigl(dR^2 + R^2\,d\Omega^2\bigr). \] Introduce the areal radius \[ r = a(t)\,R, \quad dr = a\,dR + \dot a\,R\,dt = a\,dR + H\,r\,dt, \quad H(t)\equiv\frac{\dot a}{a}. \]

Substituting \(dR=(dr-H\,r\,dt)/a\) and setting \(d\Omega=0\) gives \[ ds^2 = -\,dt^2 + \bigl(dr - H\,r\,dt\bigr)^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2 \\ = -\bigl(1-H^2r^2\bigr)dt^2 + 2H\,r\,dt\,dr + dr^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2. \] Relabeling \(t\to\tau\) then yields the PG form \[ ds^2 = -\,d\tau^2 + \bigl(dr + F(r,\tau)\,d\tau\bigr)^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2, \quad F(r,\tau)=H(\tau)\,r. \]

Direct Einstein–Tensor Derivation

Alternatively, start from the ansatz \[ ds^2 = -\,d\tau^2 + \bigl(dr + F(r,\tau)\,d\tau\bigr)^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2. \] A straightforward computation of the Einstein tensor gives \[ G_{\tau\tau} = \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{d}{dr}\bigl[r\,F(r,\tau)^2\bigr]. \]

Equating \(G_{\tau\tau}=8\pi G\,T_{\tau\tau}\) with \(\;T_{\tau\tau}=\rho_{m}\,a^{-3}(\tau)+\rho_\Lambda\) (constant on each \(\tau\!={\rm const}\) slice) yields \[ \frac{d}{dr}\bigl[r\,F^2\bigr] =8\pi G\bigl(\rho_{m}a^{-3}+\rho_\Lambda\bigr)\,r^2. \] Integrating from \(0\) to \(r\) and imposing regularity at \(r=0\), \[ r\,F^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m}a^{-3}+\rho_\Lambda\bigr)\,r^3 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad F(r,\tau)=r\,\sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m}a^{-3}(\tau)+\rho_\Lambda\bigr)}. \]

Appendix B: Israel–Darmois Junction Conditions

Timelike Shell

The matching hypersurface \(Σ\) is defined by \(r=a(\tau)\). In PG form the metric reads \[ ds^2=-d\tau^2+(dr+F\,d\tau)^2+r^2d\Omega^2, \] so \[ g^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu r\,\partial_\nu r =1-F^2(r,\tau), \] and \(Σ\) is timelike provided \(F^2 < 1\).

First Fundamental Form

On \(Σ\), \(dr/d\tau=-F\), so both interior and exterior induce \[ ds^2_\Sigma=-d\tau^2+r^2d\Omega^2, \] satisfying \(g_{ab}^{\rm int}=g_{ab}^{\rm ext}\) without further rescaling.

Second Fundamental Form

With unit normal \(n_\mu=\{ -F,1,0,0\}/\sqrt{1+F^2}\), one finds \[ K_{rr}=\partial_rF,\quad K_{\theta\theta}=rF,\quad K_{\phi\phi}=r\sin^2\!\theta\,F, \] and since \(F_{\rm int}=F_{\rm ext}\) on \(Σ\) all \(K_{ab}\) match, implying no δ‐stress.

Parameter Matching

Equating the interior shift \(F=H\,r\) with the exterior \(F=\sqrt{2GM/r+(\Lambda/3)r^2}\) gives \[ H^2r^2 = \frac{2GM}{r} + \frac{\Lambda\,r^2}{3} \;\Longrightarrow\; H^2 = \frac{2GM}{r^3} + \frac{\Lambda}{3}. \] Using \(r=a(\tau)\) and the Friedmann relation \(H^2=\tfrac{8\pi G}{3}(\rho_{m}a^{-3}+\rho_\Lambda)\) immediately fixes \(\;M=\tfrac{4\pi G}{3}\rho_{m}\) and \(\;\Lambda=8\pi G\,\rho_\Lambda\).

With both fundamental forms continuous and parameter identities satisfied, the Israel–Darmois conditions guarantee a smooth, stress-free matching. For full derivations, see Poisson & Visser [26 and Bressange [35].

Appendix C: Numerical Estimate of \(\Lambda\) from Geometric Duality

We derive the cosmological constant \(\Lambda\) from two independent geometric inputs in the geometric duality framework—first from the cosmic horizon radius, then from the universe’s age—and show they agree to within a few tens of percent.

From the Horizon Radius

The matching at \(r=1\) implies a Hubble radius \[ R_H = \frac{c}{H_0}, \quad H_0 = 70\;\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}Mpc^{-1}} \approx 2.27\times10^{-18}\,\mathrm{s^{-1}}, \] giving \[ R_H \approx \frac{3.0\times10^8\;\mathrm{m/s}}{2.27\times10^{-18}\;\mathrm{s^{-1}}} \approx 1.32\times10^{26}\;\mathrm{m}. \] The geometric duality prediction is then \[ \Lambda = \frac{3}{R_H^2} \;\approx\; \frac{3}{(1.32\times10^{26}\,\mathrm{m})^2} \approx 1.7\times10^{-52}\;\mathrm{m^{-2}}. \]

From the Universe’s Age

Equivalently, in geometric duality the shell’s proper‐time to the horizon scales like the cosmic age \(T_0\approx13.8\;\mathrm{Gyr}=4.35\times10^{17}\,\mathrm{s}\), so \[ \Lambda = \frac{3}{c^2\,T_0^2} = \frac{3}{(3.0\times10^8\,\mathrm{m/s})^2\,(4.35\times10^{17}\,\mathrm{s})^2} \approx 1.6\times10^{-52}\;\mathrm{m^{-2}}. \] These two estimates agree within \(\sim10\%\), validating the geometric input.

Corresponding Vacuum‐Energy Density

Converting \(\Lambda\) to an equivalent vacuum‐energy density via \(\rho_{\Lambda} = \Lambda\,c^2/(8\pi G)\) yields \[ \rho_{\Lambda} \approx \frac{1.7\times10^{-52}\,\mathrm{m^{-2}}\times(3.0\times10^8\,\mathrm{m/s})^2} {8\pi\times6.67\times10^{-11}\,\mathrm{m^3/kg\,s^2}} \approx 9\times10^{-27}\;\mathrm{kg/m^3}. \]

A more precise fit—including the matter contribution via \(T_0=\int_0^1da/[a\sqrt{\Omega_m a^{-3}+\Omega_\Lambda}]\)—adjusts \(\Lambda\) by \(\lesssim10\%\). For detailed cosmological fits, see standard reviews.

Appendix D: Connection to the 5D de Sitter Embedding

A striking way to see the construction in a unified geometric light is to observe that the late‐time exterior FLRW region with dust + Λ asymptotes to de Sitter space, which itself can be embedded as a 4-dimensional hyperboloid in 5-dimensional Minkowski space. Concretely:

De Sitter asymptotics: For dust + Λ in a flat FLRW patch, as \(a\to\infty\) (or \(t\gg H_{0}^{-1}\)), the matter term redshifts away and \begin{equation} H^{2}(t)\;\to\;\frac{\Lambda}{3},\quad a(t)\sim e^{\sqrt{\Lambda/3}\,t}. \end{equation} The metric becomes \begin{equation} ds^{2}\;\approx\;-\,dt^{2}+e^{2\sqrt{\Lambda/3}\,t}\,dx^{2}, \end{equation} the standard flat‐slicing of de Sitter space.

5D hyperboloid: That de Sitter geometry is isometric to the hyperboloid \begin{equation} -X_{0}^{2}+X_{1}^{2}+X_{2}^{2}+X_{3}^{2}+X_{4}^{2} = \frac{3}{\Lambda}, \end{equation} living in 5D Minkowski \((-++++\)). Time evolution \(t\mapsto t+\Delta\) is a hyperbolic “boost” in the \((X_{0},X_{4})\) plane, with boost angle \(\sqrt{\Lambda/3}\,c\,\Delta\).

Geometric Duality matching: In geometric duality we glue our interior Schwarzschild‐like region at \(r=1\) onto this dust + Λ FLRW patch. Thus our cosmic horizon in the interior corresponds precisely to the de Sitter hyperboloid’s “waist” in the 5D picture: \begin{equation} r=1\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\sqrt{X_{1}^{2}+X_{2}^{2}+X_{3}^{2}}= \sqrt{\tfrac{3}{\Lambda}}. \end{equation} The cosmological constant \(\Lambda=3/R_{H}^{2}\) is then exactly the inverse square of that hyperboloid radius, tying the geometric duality mass/age input to the embedding curvature.

In this way, geometric duality’s interior-scaling and its matched FLRW exterior fit seamlessly into the classic 5D hyperboloid embedding of de Sitter space. Dark energy becomes nothing more than the curvature radius of that hyperboloid—a geometric fact rather than an ad‐hoc parameter.
