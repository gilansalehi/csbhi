Abstract

The Universe is a Black Hole.

We propose a geometric reinterpretation of ΛCDM cosmology by embedding our observable universe inside the interior of a Schwarzschild‐like horizon at \( r = 1 \) (in units \( c=1, G=1, 2M=1 \)) and filling it with pressureless dust of density \(\rho_{m}(r)=\rho_{m,0}\,r^{3}\) plus a cosmological constant \(\rho_{\Lambda}\).

In this framework, the radial coordinate \(r(\tau)\in(0,1]\) plays the role of an effective scale factor, shrinking from \(r=1\) at cosmic time \(\tau=\tau_{0}\) to \(r\to0\) as \(\tau\to\infty\), thereby avoiding any finite-time singularity.

Adopting Painlevé–Gullstrand slicing, the interior line element and geodesic infall rate are

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\,d\tau^{2} + \bigl(dr + F(r)\,d\tau\bigr)^{2} + r^{2}\,d\Omega^{2}, \quad F(r) = \sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,r^{3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr)}. \end{equation}

Along each dust worldline,

\begin{equation} \frac{dr}{d\tau} = -\,F(r). \end{equation}

Identifying the effective scale factor as \(a=1/r\) at the horizon yields

\begin{equation} H^{2} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,a^{-3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr), \end{equation}

thereby recovering the observed Hubble constant \(H_{0}\), cosmic age \(T_{0}\approx13.8\;\mathrm{Gyr}\), and all standard distance–redshift relations \(D_{L}(z)\), \(D_{A}(z)\). Moreover, the proper-time integral

\begin{equation} \int_{r=1}^{0}\frac{dr}{F(r)} = \infty \end{equation}

pushes the central singularity to \(\tau\to\infty\). This construction suggests dark energy may emerge naturally from the interior geometry of a cosmic horizon, rather than requiring an explicit fluid with negative pressure.

You Are Here

In Euclidean geometry, Pythagoras tells us that the distance \(ds\) between two points in a Cartesian coordinate grid \((x,y)\) is given by

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = dx^{2} + dy^{2} \end{equation} [1]

This immediately generalizes to three (and higher) dimensions:

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = dx^{2} + dy^{2} + dz^{2} + \dots \end{equation}

This positive-definite quadratic form of the Euclidean metric is invariant under rotations and translations, and provides a universal standard for measuring distances.

In 1905, Einstein unified space and time in his theory of Special Relativity, replacing the simple metric with the spacetime interval:

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\,dt^{2} + dx^{2} + dy^{2} + dz^{2} \quad(c=1), \end{equation} [2]

or, for purely radial separations,

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\,dt^{2} + dr^{2}. \end{equation}

Minkowski (1908) interpreted this geometrically to show that events lie on a four-dimensional manifold with Lorentzian signature \((-,+,+,+)\), and that \(ds^{2}\) plays exactly the role of a metric distance [3]. Crucially, the negative sign on the temporal component distinguishes timelike \((ds^{2} < 0)\) from spacelike \((ds^{2} > 0)\) separations, enforcing the invariant light-cone structure that governs causality and underpinning the hallmark relativistic effects of time dilation and length contraction.

Einstein’s theory of General Relativity (1915) promotes the metric \(g_{\mu\nu}(x)\) to a dynamical field. Free-falling particles follow geodesics determined by the Christoffel symbols (first derivatives of \(g\)), while the manifold’s curvature—encoded in the Riemann and Ricci tensors (second derivatives of \(g\))—comes from energy and momentum. This relationship is described in Einstein's field equations:

\begin{equation} G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac12\,R\,g_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi\,T_{\mu\nu} \quad(c=G=1). \end{equation} [4]

In 1916 Schwarzschild found the first unique spherically symmetric vacuum solution of these field equations, describing a Black Hole:

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\Bigl(1 - \frac{2M}{r}\Bigr)\,dt^{2} + \Bigl(1 - \frac{2M}{r}\Bigr)^{-1}\,dr^{2} + r^{2}\,d\Omega^{2}. \end{equation} [5]

Adopting conventional units where \(2M=1\) locates the Black Hole's Event Horizon at \(r=1\). If we only consider radial motion, the \(d\Omega\) term goes to 0, leaving just

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\Bigl(1 - \frac{1}{r}\Bigr)\,dt^{2} + \Bigl(1 - \frac{1}{r}\Bigr)^{-1}\,dr^{2} \end{equation}

Above the Event Horizon (\( r > 1 \)), the factor \(1 - 1/r\) is positive, so \(t\) remains a timelike coordinate and \(r\) remains spacelike, in accordance with our usual notion of time and space, but below the Event Horizon (\( r < 1 \)), the factor \(1 - 1/r\) becomes negative, flipping the signs in front of \(dt^{2}\) and \(dr^{2}\) and thus exchanging the roles of \(t\) and \(r\): the radial direction becomes timelike and the timelike direction becomes spacelike.

But what does it mean for space and time to exchange roles at the horizon?

ΛCDM Model: Predictions & Limitations

But first: what does it mean for the coordinate roles to be fixed? In standard cosmology we enforce a global comoving time parameter \(t\) that remains timelike everywhere, so space and time never swap roles. This gives the spatially flat FLRW line element:

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\,dt^{2} + a(t)^{2}\bigl(dR^{2} + R^{2}\,d\Omega^{2}\bigr). \end{equation} [6]

The expansion dynamics follow from the Friedmann equations (1923):

\begin{equation} H^{2} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m} + \rho_{r} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr), \quad \frac{\ddot a}{a} = -\frac{4\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m} + 2\rho_{r} - 2\rho_{\Lambda}\bigr), \end{equation}

where \(\rho_{m}\propto a^{-3}\), \(\rho_{r}\propto a^{-4}\), and \(\rho_{\Lambda}=\Lambda/(8\pi G)\).

ΛCDM excels at matching:

  • Type Ia supernovae indicating late‐time acceleration. [9]
  • Baryon Acoustic Oscillation scales, [10]
  • Cosmic Microwave Background anisotropies, [11]
  • Hubble expansion rate (\(H_{0}\approx67\text{–}73\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}\,Mpc^{-1}}\)), [11, 12]

Yet ΛCDM faces deep conceptual and observational challenges:

  • Cosmological constant problem: Quantum‐field estimates of \(\rho_{\Lambda}\) exceed observations by 50+ orders of magnitude.
  • Fine-tuning: Why is \(\rho_{\Lambda}\) nonzero yet so extraordinarily small?
  • Coincidence problem: Why do we happen to live at the epoch where \(\Omega_{m}\approx\Omega_{\Lambda}\)?
  • No direct detection: Dark energy interacts only via gravity, with no nongravitational signature.
  • Hubble tension: A persistent discrepancy between local distance‐ladder and CMB‐inferred values of \(H_{0}\). [11, 12]
  • Inevitable singularities: Hawking–Penrose theorems guarantee classical GR produces spacetime singularities under reasonable conditions. [13]

By fixing the comoving slicing, ΛCDM sidesteps the coordinate‐role swap seen at a black‐hole horizon. Is it possible that ΛCDM is overlooking something of great significance?

A Leap of Faith (Gedenkenexperiment)

To conceptualize the coordinate switch, let us consider physics’ two most famous astronauts, Alice and Bob, in just the scenario Schwarzschild describes. Their space station is in deep space, where the spacetime curvature is negligible, observing a supermassive black hole (SMBH) from a super‐safe distance. For now, let us say they are hovering far above the horizon, at \(r\gg 1\) (in conventional units where \(r=2M=1\)) with thrusters. On the station they experience inertial motion, and by the Equivalence Principle [14] they measure space to be flat in all directions. Alice departs in the G‐Odyssic, a spacecraft built to survive the journey to the black hole interior, and maneuvers until the SMBH’s gravity pulls her in.

Initially, Alice and Bob are very close (both at \(r\gg 1\)), and their relative velocity is small. Alice sees Bob and the station receding in her rearview cameras, eventually shrinking into a single point of light in the sky behind her, while she lets gravity pull the G‐Odyssic straight down. Meanwhile, Bob sees Alice begin to drift inward and fall toward the SMBH, accelerating as she does. Bob knows two things from General Relativity: [18]

  • In his own coordinate time \(t\), he will never see Alice cross the horizon at \(r=1\), nor receive any signal from her once she does. As \(t\to\infty\), her light and radio pulses become infinitely redshifted and fade away.
  • In Alice’s proper time \(\tau\), she will reach \( r=1 \) after a finite interval and then continue inward in free fall.
Because Alice's motion remains inertial during the entire journey, she registers no forces. In her local frame, her clock ticks normally, and she measures space as flat. But what she experiences versus what Bob sees are starkly different.

As Alice free‐falls toward the horizon, her radial coordinate \(r(\tau)\) decreases, and her speed relative to Bob increases. The black hole grows larger and larger in her viewport. In the unit‐circle diagram, Alice slowly approaches the event horizon located at \(r=1\)—see Figure 2.

At the precise moment Alice’s coordinate reaches \(r=1\), Bob’s reception interval between successive radio pulses stretches to infinity—he sees her signal smear across the event horizon. To him, Alice asymptotically approaches \(r=1\) as \(t\to+\infty\). Yet in Alice’s local frame, crossing \(r=1\) is unremarkable: she registers no sudden forces, space appears locally flat, and her proper‐time \(\tau\) continues smoothly, as required by the Equivalence Principle. In the unit‐circle diagram, she simply passes through the perimeter—see Figure 3.

As Alice moves past \(r=1\), Bob’s station vanishes from her instruments entirely— his radio pulses have redshifted to zero. In fact, all light from above the horizon shifts from red to black. Yet aboard the freely-falling G-Odyssic, nothing changes: her local spacetime remains perfectly flat (Minkowskian), just as the Equivalence Principle demands.

Below the horizon, Alice is in uncharted territory. According to GR, in pure‐vacuum Schwarzschild, Alice’s proper‐time to reach the central singularity at \(r=0\) is finite:

\begin{equation} \tau_{\text{vac}} = \int_{1}^{0} \frac{dr}{\sqrt{1/r}} = \tfrac{2}{3} \quad(\text{in }2M=1\text{ units}) \end{equation}

But this pure-vacuum picture doesn’t account for the coordinate switch, and leads to an inevitable singularity at \(r=0\). Is it possible to conceive the Black Hole interior in such a way that Alice doesn’t crash into the singularity? And if she doesn’t hit it at \( r=0 \), where is it? Fortunately, we already have the mental tools to intuitively grasp the interior geometry of the Black Hole. All we need is a little perspective—see Figure 6.

Just as a driver will never reach the road's vanishing point, where parallel lines appear to intersect, so too will Alice never reach the singularity, where all geodesics appear to converge.

A pure-vacuum interior, as Schwarzschild envisioned, forces a real crash at \(r=0\) in finite \( \tau \). This is where we depart from the standard story: to preserve Alice’s inertial experience all the way down, we replace the vacuum with a dust + Λ Painlevé-Gullstrand interior metric, characterized by the infall function \(F(r)\). In that geometry, the proper‐time to \(r=0\) becomes

\begin{equation} \omega = \int_{0}^{1} \frac{dr}{F(r)}, \quad F(r) = \sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl[\rho_{m,0}r^{3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr]}. \end{equation} [15, 16]

Because \(F(r)\sim r\sqrt{\rho_{\Lambda}}\) near \(r=0\), \(\omega\to\infty\), Alice never reaches a singularity in finite proper time—her journey remains inertial, and her local space stays flat at every moment, as required by the Equivalence Principle. The coordinate switch means the black hole singularity is not a point in space, but a point in time located at future infinity.

Furthermore, the specific geometry of this novel perspective has some very surprising qualities.

Painlevé–Gullstrand Coordinates

To track a freely falling observer across the horizon \( r=1 \) without a coordinate singularity, we use the Painlevé–Gullstrand form:

$$ds^{2} = -\,d\tau^{2} + \bigl(dr + \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{r}}\,d\tau\bigr)^{2} + r^{2}d\Omega^{2}$$ [15, 16]

which is manifestly regular at \( r=1 \) and covers both interior and exterior regions. See also: [17, 18]

Dust + \( \Lambda \) Interior Ansatz

Motivated by Lemaître–Tolman–Bondi collapse models with \( \Lambda \) [19, 20, 21], we take the interior metric in Painlevé–Gullstrand form and posit

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\,d\tau^{2} + \bigl(dr + F(r)\,d\tau\bigr)^{2} + r^{2}\,d\Omega^{2} \end{equation}

with

\begin{equation} F(r) = \sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,r^{3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr)}. \end{equation}

This ansatz exactly solves the Einstein equation \(\displaystyle G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G\,T_{\mu\nu}\) for a perfect fluid with density \(\rho_{m}(r)=\rho_{m,0}\,r^{3}\) plus vacuum energy \(\rho_{\Lambda}\) [17, 18]. Along each dust worldline,

\begin{equation} \frac{dr}{d\tau} = -\,F(r). \end{equation}

Matching to FLRW & Recovery of Friedmann

Note: In this section, \( T \) denotes the cosmic FLRW time coordinate (the argument of the scale factor \(a(T)\)), not to be confused with (Bob's) Schwarzschild time \( t \) or (Alice's) proper time \( \tau \).

We glue the dust + Λ interior to an exterior flat FLRW region by identifying the interior horizon at \( r=1 \) with an exterior 2-sphere whose areal radius is also 1. The exterior metric is

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\,dT^{2} + a(T)^{2}\bigl(dR^{2} + R^{2}d\Omega^{2}\bigr). \end{equation} [6, 7, 8]

where R is the comoving radial coordinate in the FLRW patch.

At the matching time \(T_{0}\) we set \(a(T_{0})=1\) and \(R=1\), ensuring the induced metric on the 2-sphere agrees on both sides of the junction.

Continuity of the 2-sphere metric is automatic; continuity of the extrinsic curvature follows from the Israel–Darmois junction conditions. A concise derivation (Appendix B) shows that matching the lapse across the hypersurface gives

\begin{equation} d\tau \;=\; \frac{dT}{\sqrt{\,1 - H_{0}^{2}\,}}, \qquad H_{0}^{2} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr), \end{equation} [22]

which reproduces the standard relation between the interior proper time \(\tau\) and cosmic time \(T\).

Finally, identifying the interior scale factor \(a=1/r\) on the matching surface and using \(\tfrac{dr}{d\tau}=-F(r)\) yields

\begin{equation} H(a) \;=\; \frac{1}{a}\frac{da}{dT} = -\,\frac{1}{r}\frac{dr}{d\tau} = \sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,r^{3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr)} = \sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,a^{-3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr)}, \end{equation}

thereby exactly recovering the Friedmann equation for a spatially flat ΛCDM universe.

Observables

From above we have the exact flat–ΛCDM Friedmann equation \begin{equation} H(a)^{2} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,a^{-3}+\rho_{\Lambda}\bigr), \quad H(a)\equiv\frac{1}{a}\frac{da}{dT}. \end{equation} [6]

Hubble constant:

At \(a=1\),

\begin{equation} H_{0} = \sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}+\rho_{\Lambda}\bigr)}, \end{equation}

matching the observed \(H_{0}\approx67\text{–}73\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}Mpc^{-1}}\) [11, 12].

Cosmic age:

\begin{equation} T_{0} = \int_{0}^{1}\frac{da}{a\,H(a)} \approx 13.8\;\mathrm{Gyr}, \end{equation} in agreement with standard ΛCDM fits [11].

Distance–redshift relations:

Defining \(1+z=1/a\), the luminosity and angular‐diameter distances are \begin{equation} D_{L}(z) = (1+z)\int_{0}^{z}\frac{dz'}{H(z')}, \quad D_{A}(z)=\frac{1}{1+z}\int_{0}^{z}\frac{dz'}{H(z')}, \end{equation} exactly reproducing supernova, BAO and CMB distance measures [25].

Discussion

Conceptual Interpretation

Accepting this conformal geometry for Black Hole Interiors does a lot more than provide asymptotic safety for Alice's worldline. It has serious implications for our understanding of space, time, and the universe.

In the standard ΛCDM paradigm, the cosmological constant is introduced as an extra term in Einstein’s equations purely to fit the observed late-time acceleration [33]. Although it provides an excellent phenomenological match to supernova, CMB and large-scale-structure data, Λ remains an ad-hoc parameter without deeper geometric origin [24].

CSBHI turns this model inside-out. By filling a Schwarzschild-like horizon with pressureless dust plus a constant energy density and insisting on a smooth Painlevé–Gullstrand slicing, one inevitably recovers the very same Λ term in the unique infall function. When that interior is matched to an exterior FLRW universe via \(a=1/r\), the resulting expansion history exactly reproduces the flat ΛCDM Friedmann evolution—and all the key observables (Hubble rate, cosmic age, distance–redshift relations) follow without invoking any extra dark-energy fluid.

This shift from treating Λ as an empirical adjustment to deriving geometrically it from first‐principles yields several immediate conceptual benefits:

  • Singularity resolution: The proper-time to the center diverges, so what would have been a curvature singularity becomes a “future endpoint” at \(\tau\to\infty\), preserving inertial continuity.
  • Natural scale setting: Matching the horizon at \(r=1\) to \(a=1\) fixes the overall normalization of cosmic expansion—no arbitrary rescaling of the scale factor is needed.
  • Coincidence explained: Observers crossing the horizon always encounter the moment \(\rho_{m}=\rho_{\Lambda}\) at \(a=1\), so \(\Omega_{m}\approx\Omega_{\Lambda}\) arises as a geometric inevitability rather than fine-tuning.
  • Geometric origin of acceleration: Cosmic acceleration arises because the interior geometry enforces an infall rate \(F(r)\) that naturally switches from matter‐dominated deceleration (\(\rho_{m}\propto a^{-3}\)) to Λ‐driven acceleration at late times.
  • Unified framework: The same conformal scaling that governs free-fall inside a black-hole interior also governs the universe’s expansion, unifying two seemingly disparate regimes into a single metric framework.

By explaining dark energy via the conformal geometry of a cosmic horizon rather than in a mysterious fluid, CSBHI offers a first-principles explanation for cosmic acceleration, reframes the cosmological-constant problem, and suggests new avenues for understanding singularities and horizon thermodynamics. This geometric paradigm unifies the notions of cosmic expansion and gravitational collapse under a single framework.

Responses to Objections

A few technical concerns warrant comment before proceeding.

  • Horizon‐entropy subtleties: The standard Bekenstein–Hawking area law applies to stationary (vacuum) horizons [28, 29]. In our dynamical dust + Λ interior, one should use the Ashtekar–Krishnan framework for dynamical horizons, which includes matter‐flux contributions and obeys a generalized second law [27]. We defer a full thermodynamic analysis to future work, noting here only that the total entropy (horizon + matter) satisfies a generalized area‐increase law.
  • Junction stability: Israel–Darmois matching at the \(r=1\) hypersurface yields zero surface stress. Linear‐perturbation analyses (e.g. Poisson & Visser, [26]) show that small shell‐fluctuations remain bounded, ensuring a stable gluing to the exterior FLRW region.
  • Perturbations & structure formation: Though we assume spherical symmetry at the background level, dust + Λ Lemaître–Tolman–Bondi perturbations reproduce the standard ΛCDM linear growth factor [24, 30]. A detailed study of non‐linear structure and observational signatures is left for future work.

Limitations

CSBHI has made a number of simplifying assumptions:

  • Spherical symmetry: We have assumed an exactly spherically symmetric dust shell. Generic collapse will feature anisotropies, caustics, or shell crossings before \(r=1\). Numerical‐relativity studies are required to assess the stability of our picture under realistic perturbations.
  • Neglect of radiation & pressure: Our interior source is pure dust + Λ. Early‐universe radiation (\(\rho_{r}\propto a^{-4}\)) or baryonic pressure could modify \(F(r)\) near the junction and shift the effective \(H_{0}\). Incorporating these components remains for future work.
  • Perturbation matching: We have matched only the homogeneous background. Extending this to cosmological perturbations—matching metric and matter fluctuations across the \(r=1\) hypersurface in a consistent gauge—is a nontrivial technical challenge.
  • Quantum Mechanics: We omit all quantum and semiclassical effects (vacuum fluctuations, horizon-entropy, particle creation) that, at Planck or horizon scales, can modify the interior geometry and shift Λ. Accounting for these corrections requires extending QM with new geometric tools, which we leave to future work.

Future Directions

We highlight three high-priority avenues for extending and testing the CSBHI framework:

  • Cosmological perturbations: Derive how primordial scalar and tensor modes propagate across the \(r=1\) junction, compute the resulting CMB and large-scale-structure power spectra, and compare directly with Planck and upcoming survey data.
  • Horizon thermodynamics: Develop a full entropy and energy-flux balance law for the dynamical dust + Λ horizon using the Ashtekar–Krishnan framework, and explore its implications for horizon microphysics and the generalized second law.
  • Accretion-driven mass evolution & Hubble tension: Investigate whether net mass flow from the exterior FLRW region—via matter accretion across the \(r=1\) matching surface—can produce an effective change in the interior’s horizon mass of order 10%, and thus alleviate the local vs. CMB \(H_{0}\) discrepancy.

Conclusion

By interpreting our observable universe as the interior of a cosmic‐scale Schwarzschild horizon at \(r=1\), we have shown that all principal features of ΛCDM follow directly from conformal geometry. In particular, the identification

\begin{equation} a = \frac{1}{r} \end{equation}

together with the infall law

\begin{equation} \frac{dr}{d\tau} = -\,F(r) \end{equation}

yields the exact Friedmann evolution

\begin{equation} H^{2} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,a^{-3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr) \end{equation}

reproducing the observed Hubble constant \(H_{0}\), cosmic age \(T_{0}\approx13.8\;\mathrm{Gyr}\), distance–redshift relations, and CMB/BAO shift parameters.

  • Geometric dark energy: Cosmic acceleration emerges from the interior conformal geometry rather than an ad-hoc fluid.
  • Coincidence naturally explained: Matching at \(r=1\) fixes \(a=1\) at the epoch \(\rho_{m}=\rho_{\Lambda}\), so \(\Omega_{m}\approx\Omega_{\Lambda}\) today requires no fine-tuning.
  • Singularity deferred: The central curvature singularity at \(r=0\) is pushed to \(\tau\to\infty\), opening paths toward non-singular interior bounces.

We have laid the groundwork for a unified geometric paradigm that ties gravitational collapse and cosmic expansion into a single framework. We anticipate that future work on perturbation matching, dynamical-horizon thermodynamics, and horizon accretion dynamics will further clarify and test this framework. Ultimately, this construction unifies gravitational collapse and cosmic expansion under a single geometric theory, offering fresh perspective into the nature of dark energy, cosmic inflation, and the universe.

Appendix B: Israel–Darmois Junction Conditions

We match the interior dust + Λ metric in Painlevé–Gullstrand form to the exterior flat FLRW metric across the timelike hypersurface Σ defined by \(r=1\) (interior) and \(a(T)\,R=1\) (exterior). The induced metrics on Σ are

\begin{equation} ds^{2}_{\Sigma}\bigl|_{\text{int}} = -\bigl[\,1 - H_{0}^{2}\bigr]\,d\tau^{2} + d\Omega^{2}, \quad ds^{2}_{\Sigma}\bigl|_{\text{ext}} = -\,dT^{2} + d\Omega^{2}. \end{equation}

To match these, introduce a lapse factor \(\alpha\) so that

\begin{equation} \alpha^{2}\,\bigl[\,1 - H_{0}^{2}\bigr]\,d\tau^{2} = dT^{2}. \end{equation}

Continuity of the extrinsic curvature across Σ is imposed via the Israel–Darmois conditions†, which require \(\alpha\,[1 - H_{0}^{2}]=1\). Hence

\begin{equation} \alpha = \frac{1}{\,1 - H_{0}^{2}\,} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad d\tau = \frac{dT}{\sqrt{\,1 - H_{0}^{2}\,}}. \end{equation}

This relation ensures that interior proper time \(\tau\) and exterior cosmic time \(T\) coincide on Σ, with no delta‐function stress‐energy layer.

Appendix C: Friedmann Equation (Standard vs. Our Form)

The most general Friedmann equation for a FLRW universe (with speed of light \(c=1\)) is

\begin{equation} H^{2} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\,\rho - \frac{k}{a^{2}} + \frac{\Lambda}{3} \quad\text{([6])}. \end{equation}

For a spatially flat universe (\(k=0\)), one often writes the \(\Lambda\)-term as a vacuum‐energy density

\begin{equation} \rho_{\Lambda} \equiv \frac{\Lambda}{8\pi G} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{\Lambda}{3} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\,\rho_{\Lambda}. \end{equation}

Substituting into the flat–\(k\) equation gives the form we use throughout:

\begin{equation} H^{2} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\,\bigl(\rho + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr) \quad(k=0). \end{equation}

In our model, \(\rho\) consists of matter only (\(\rho_{m}\)). Hence

\begin{equation} H^{2} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\,\bigl(\rho_{m} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr), \end{equation}

exactly matching the standard ΛCDM expression once you set \(k=0\) and identify \(\rho_{m}\) and \(\rho_{\Lambda}\).

\begin{equation} H(a) = \sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,a^{-3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr)} \end{equation}

In a spatially flat FLRW universe, the standard distance measures are:

  • Luminosity distance: \begin{equation} D_{L}(z) = (1 + z)\,\int_{0}^{z}\frac{dz'}{H(z')}\, \end{equation}
  • Angular‐diameter distance: \begin{equation} D_{A}(z) = \frac{1}{1 + z}\,\int_{0}^{z}\frac{dz'}{H(z')}\, \end{equation}

References: [6, 23, 30]

Appendix E: Distance Continuity & Infinite‐Time Integral

As shown in Appendix B, physical distances match continuously across the matching surface Σ at \(r=1\), since

\begin{equation} D_{\mathrm{int}} = r\,\Delta\theta,\quad D_{\mathrm{ext}} = a\,\Delta R,\quad a = \frac{1}{r},\quad \Delta R = \Delta\theta, \end{equation}

which implies \(D_{\mathrm{int}} = D_{\mathrm{ext}}\).

The proper‐time for an infalling observer to reach \(r=0\) is

\begin{equation} \Delta\tau = \int_{r=1}^{0} \frac{dr}{F(r)} = \int_{0}^{1} \frac{dr}{\sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,r^{3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr)}} \end{equation}

Near \(r\to0\), one has \(F(r)\approx r\sqrt{\tfrac{8\pi G}{3}\,\rho_{\Lambda}}\), so the integral diverges:

\begin{equation} \int_{0}^{\delta} \frac{dr}{F(r)} \;\approx\; \int_{0}^{\delta} \frac{dr}{r\sqrt{8\pi G\,\rho_{\Lambda}/3}} = \infty, \end{equation}

demonstrating that no observer reaches \(r=0\) in finite proper time. Similar divergent behavior appears in Lemaître–Tolman–Bondi collapse models.

See: [19, 20, 21].

Appendix F: Derivation of the Infall Function \(F(r)\)

NOTE: The radial infall (or “shift”) function

\begin{equation} F(r)=\sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,r^{3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr)} \end{equation}

is not novel to this work. It follows directly from rewriting a flat FLRW cosmology in Painlevé–Gullstrand coordinates [31] and is equivalent to the result obtained by solving the Einstein equations in PG form [18, 19, 20, 21, 32].

From FLRW to Painlevé–Gullstrand

Begin with the flat FLRW metric (\(c=1\)):

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\,dt^{2} + a(t)^{2}\bigl(dR^{2} + R^{2}\,d\Omega^{2}\bigr) \end{equation} [6, 7, 8]

Define the areal radius \(r = a(t)\,R\), so that

\begin{equation} dr = a\,dR + \dot{a}\,R\,dt = a\,dR + H\,r\,dt \quad\Longrightarrow\quad dR = \frac{dr - H\,r\,dt}{a}\,. \end{equation}

Substitute into the line element and expand:

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} ds^{2} &= -\,dt^{2} + a^{2}\Bigl(\frac{dr - H\,r\,dt}{a}\Bigr)^{2} + r^{2}\,d\Omega^{2}\\ &= -\,dt^{2} + (dr - H\,r\,dt)^{2} + r^{2}\,d\Omega^{2}\\ &= -\,dt^{2} + \bigl(dr + H\,r\,dt\bigr)^{2} + r^{2}\,d\Omega^{2}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

Comparing with the Painlevé–Gullstrand form \(ds^{2}=-d\tau^{2}+(dr+F\,d\tau)^{2}+r^{2}d\Omega^{2}\) (and setting \(\tau=t\)) gives

\begin{equation} F(r) = H(t)\,r = r\,\sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,a^{-3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr)} \quad\text{with }a=\frac{1}{r}, \end{equation}

hence

\begin{equation} F(r) = \sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,r^{3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr)}. \end{equation}

See: [6, 7, 8, 31]

Direct Einstein–Tensor Derivation

Start from the Painlevé–Gullstrand ansatz:

\begin{equation} ds^{2} = -\,d\tau^{2} + \bigl(dr + F(r)\,d\tau\bigr)^{2} + r^{2}\,d\Omega^{2}. \end{equation}

One finds the \(\tau\tau\) component of the Einstein tensor is

\begin{equation} G_{\tau\tau} = \frac{1}{r^{2}}\frac{d}{dr}\bigl[r\,F(r)^{2}\bigr]. \end{equation}

Equating to the dust + Λ stress–energy \(T_{\tau\tau}=\rho_{m,0}r^{3}+\rho_{\Lambda}\) via \(G_{\tau\tau}=8\pi G\,T_{\tau\tau}\) gives

\begin{equation} \frac{d}{dr}\bigl[r\,F(r)^{2}\bigr] = 8\pi G\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,r^{3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr). \end{equation}

Integrating from \(0\) to \(r\) (and requiring regularity at \(r=0\)) yields

\begin{equation} r\,F(r)^{2} = \int_{0}^{r}8\pi G\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,r'^{3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr)\,dr' = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,r^{4} + \rho_{\Lambda}\,r\bigr), \end{equation}

and therefore

\begin{equation} F(r)^{2} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,r^{3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr), \quad F(r) = \sqrt{\frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl(\rho_{m,0}\,r^{3} + \rho_{\Lambda}\bigr)}. \end{equation}

See: [18, 32]

Appendix G: Numerical Estimates of \(\Lambda\) from CSBHI

These numerical estimates confirm that CSBHI not only reproduces the form of the ΛCDM Friedmann equation but also predicts the observed magnitude of \(\Lambda\) from the single geometric input of the universe’s horizon size or age.

Derivation from the Cosmic Horizon Radius

In CSBHI the matching at \(r=1\) implies a Schwarzschild‐horizon radius

\begin{equation} R_{H} = \frac{c}{H_{0}}, \end{equation}

which in SI units with \(H_{0}=70\;\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}\,Mpc^{-1}}\approx2.27\times10^{-18}\,\mathrm{s^{-1}}\) gives

\begin{equation} R_{H}\approx\frac{3.0\times10^{8}\,\mathrm{m/s}}{2.27\times10^{-18}\,\mathrm{s^{-1}}} \approx1.32\times10^{26}\,\mathrm{m}. \end{equation}

The CSBHI prediction for the cosmological constant is

\begin{equation} \Lambda = \frac{3}{R_{H}^{2}} \;\approx\; \frac{3}{(1.32\times10^{26}\,\mathrm{m})^{2}} \approx1.7\times10^{-52}\,\mathrm{m^{-2}}. \end{equation}

See: [11, 12]

Derivation from the Universe’s Age

Equivalently, since in CSBHI the proper‐time to the horizon satisfies \(R_{H}\approx c\,T_{0}\), one may write

\begin{equation} \Lambda = \frac{3}{c^{2}\,T_{0}^{2}} \end{equation}

where \(T_{0}\approx13.8\;\mathrm{Gyr}\approx4.35\times10^{17}\,\mathrm{s}\). Hence

\begin{equation} \Lambda \approx \frac{3}{(3.0\times10^{8}\,\mathrm{m/s})^{2}\,(4.35\times10^{17}\,\mathrm{s})^{2}} \approx1.6\times10^{-52}\,\mathrm{m^{-2}}, \end{equation}

in excellent agreement with the horizon‐radius estimate above.

See: [11]

Corresponding Vacuum‐Energy Density

Converting \(\Lambda\) to an energy‐density via \(\rho_{\Lambda}=\Lambda c^{2}/(8\pi G)\):

\begin{equation} \rho_{\Lambda} \approx \frac{1.7\times10^{-52}\,\mathrm{m^{-2}}\times(3.0\times10^{8}\,\mathrm{m/s})^{2}} {8\pi\times6.67\times10^{-11}\,\mathrm{m^{3}/kg\,s^{2}}} \approx9\times10^{-27}\,\mathrm{kg/m^{3}}. \end{equation}

See: [23]

Appendix H: Connection to the 5D de Sitter Embedding

A striking way to see the CSBHI construction in a unified geometric light is to observe that the late‐time exterior FLRW region with dust + Λ asymptotes to de Sitter space, which itself can be embedded as a 4-dimensional hyperboloid in 5-dimensional Minkowski space. Concretely:

De Sitter asymptotics: For dust + Λ in a flat FLRW patch, as \(a\to\infty\) (or \(t\gg H_{0}^{-1}\)), the matter term redshifts away and \begin{equation} H^{2}(t)\;\to\;\frac{\Lambda}{3},\quad a(t)\sim e^{\sqrt{\Lambda/3}\,t}. \end{equation} The metric becomes \begin{equation} ds^{2}\;\approx\;-\,dt^{2}+e^{2\sqrt{\Lambda/3}\,t}\,d\mathbf{x}^{2}, \end{equation} the standard flat‐slicing of de Sitter space.

5D hyperboloid: That de Sitter geometry is isometric to the hyperboloid \begin{equation} -X_{0}^{2}+X_{1}^{2}+X_{2}^{2}+X_{3}^{2}+X_{4}^{2} = \frac{3}{\Lambda}, \end{equation} living in 5D Minkowski \((-++++\)). Time evolution \(t\mapsto t+\Delta\) is a hyperbolic “boost” in the \((X_{0},X_{4})\) plane, with boost angle \(\sqrt{\Lambda/3}\,c\,\Delta\).

CSBHI matching: In CSBHI we glue our interior Schwarzschild‐like region at \(r=1\) onto this dust + Λ FLRW patch. Thus our cosmic horizon in the interior corresponds precisely to the de Sitter hyperboloid’s “waist” in the 5D picture: \begin{equation} r=1\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\sqrt{X_{1}^{2}+X_{2}^{2}+X_{3}^{2}}= \sqrt{\tfrac{3}{\Lambda}}. \end{equation} The cosmological constant \(\Lambda=3/R_{H}^{2}\) is then exactly the inverse square of that hyperboloid radius, tying the CSBHI mass/age input to the embedding curvature.

In this way, CSBHI’s conformal‐scaling interior and its matched FLRW exterior fit seamlessly into the classic 5D hyperboloid embedding of de Sitter space. Dark energy becomes nothing more than the curvature radius of that hyperboloid—a geometric fact rather than an ad‐hoc parameter.
